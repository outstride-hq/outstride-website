## Post 1 — The hospital bed (personal story, energy is job one)

Two days after cancer surgery, I was answering investor emails from a hospital bed.

I told myself it was commitment.

Nobody on my cap table asked me to do it. Nobody would have blinked if I'd gone dark for two weeks. I did it because somewhere along the way I'd learned that a founder's job is to be available, always — and it had genuinely never occurred to me that recovery was part of the job.

Here's what I understand now, coaching founders through the same pattern:

As your company grows, your job quietly changes. Less doing, more deciding. And decisions are brutal about energy in a way tasks are not.

Write code at 70% capacity and you lose a day.
Choose the wrong quarter focus at 70% capacity and everyone loses three months.

That's why managing your own energy is close to job number one. Not self-care around the job. The job.

The system I wish I'd had:

1. Score your burnout out of 10, daily. Ten seconds inside your morning routine.
2. Pre-agree the thresholds — while you're well. Above 8: stop, take a half day minimum. Between 6 and 8: keep working, but the weekend is fully offline.
3. Tell your cofounder your triggers and your promises. "Did you book that vacation?" should carry the same weight as "did you ship that project?"

You don't get to skip recovery. You only get to choose whether it happens on your schedule or your body's.

## Post 2 — The Two Equilibria Map (tool post)

You don't have one default state. You have two — and they're both stable.

I map this with founders as the Two Equilibria.

The good equilibrium: you plan the week, take real breaks, train, sleep. Each habit feeds the next. Staying there costs you almost nothing.

The bad equilibrium: reactive all day, running on stress, caffeine to get going, a drink to switch off. Those feed each other just as reliably.

That's the trap. The bad state isn't a dip you bounce back from. It's an equilibrium. It holds itself in place — which is why "I'll feel better next week" almost never comes true.

Two forces move you between them:

Triggers push you over. And they stack — a conference, plus travel, plus hotels, plus caffeine, plus late drinks is a different beast than any one alone. A fundraise counts too. Know yours.

Recovery moves pull you back. And they must be decided in advance, because from inside the bad equilibrium you will not invent them. A two-hour slot for a long run. A spa day. A weekend where the laptop stays shut.

The map takes twenty minutes:

1. Describe your two states in behaviours, not moods.
2. List what tips you over.
3. Pre-plan the moves that bring you back — written down, bookable.
4. Share it with your cofounder and hold each other to it.

Most founders can tell me their runway to the month. Almost none can tell me which equilibrium they're in today.

## Post 3 — Marathon with sprints (short, punchy)

A startup is neither a marathon nor a sprint.

It's a marathon with periods of sprinting.

Sometimes the sprint is exactly right: a critical launch, a fundraise, the deal that changes everything. Intensity isn't the enemy.

The enemy is the sprint with no end date.

Sprinting continuously isn't commitment — it's burnout on a payment plan. You're not avoiding the cost, you're deferring it at interest. And the bill always arrives at the worst possible time.

So before your next sprint, decide two things:

— when it ends
— what recovery is booked on the other side

If you can't answer both, you're not sprinting. You're just running out of road.

## Video script — 60–90s (phase 2 format: hook → before/after → where founders get it wrong)

**HOOK (0–10s)**
Two days after cancer surgery, I was answering investor emails from a hospital bed. I thought that was commitment. It was actually the most expensive mistake I made as a founder.

**THE IDEA (10–35s)**
Every founder has two stable states. In the good equilibrium you plan, you take breaks, you exercise — and each habit reinforces the others. In the bad one you're reactive, wired, drinking to switch off — and those reinforce each other too. That's the trap: you don't drift back to good on your own. Something has to pull you back.

**BEFORE / AFTER (35–55s)**
In the bad equilibrium you make quarter-defining decisions at 70% capacity and call it commitment. Founders who've mapped their states can name exactly what tips them over — a conference, a fundraise, five nights in hotels — and they have recovery moves pre-planned and booked: the long run, the spa day, the offline weekend.

**WHERE FOUNDERS GET IT WRONG (55–80s)**
Three mistakes. Treating recovery as a reward for finishing — the work never finishes. Running the whole startup as one long sprint — it's a marathon with periods of sprinting. And keeping the struggle private from your cofounder, so nobody catches the slide until it shows up in your decisions.

**CTA (80–90s)**
Start stupidly small: score your burnout out of ten, every day. Above an eight, you stop. Ten seconds a day — and it might be the highest-leverage habit you own.
